Response:
#ml #symbolic_regression #concept_drift #model_monitoring #evaluation
1. Problem framing
You are effectively treating symbolic regression as a structural model discovery process over time.
At each time step $t$ (e.g. daily):
New dataset: $D_t = {(x_i, y_i)}_{i=1}^{n_t}$
Fit symbolic regression
Obtain formula:
$$
f_t(x) = \text{symbolic_model}(D_t)
$$
The objective is to determine whether the underlying functional relationship
$$
y = f(x)
$$
has changed over time.
Concept drift in this setting means:
$$
f_t(x) \neq f_{t-k}(x)
$$
in either:
structure (different symbolic expression)
parameters (same expression but coefficients differ)
---
2. Types of drift in symbolic regression
Symbolic models allow detection of two distinct drift types.
Structural drift
Change in expression tree:
Example:
$$
f_{t-1}(x) = 3x_1 + 0.5x_2
$$
$$
f_t(x) = 3x_1 + 0.5x_2 + \log(x_3)
$$
Interpretation:
new variable influence
new nonlinear relationship
---
Parametric drift
Structure constant but coefficients shift.
Example:
$$
f_{t-1}(x) = 3x_1 + 0.5x_2
$$
$$
f_t(x) = 2.6x_1 + 0.7x_2
$$
Interpretation:
same governing relationship
different magnitudes.
---
3. Basic monitoring pipeline
A practical pipeline.
Step 1 — fit daily symbolic model
For each day:
$$
f_t = SR(D_t)
$$
Example libraries:
PySR
gplearn
Operon
Store:
expression tree
coefficients
training error
---
Step 2 — evaluate historical performance
Evaluate new formula on previous datasets.
$$
E_{t,k} = \text{error}(f_t, D_{t-k})
$$
Example:
model	data	error
$f_t$	$D_t$	0.05
$f_t$	$D_{t-1}$	0.30
$f_{t-1}$	$D_t$	0.28
Large cross-error indicates drift.
---
Step 3 — expression similarity
Compute structural similarity between expressions.
Methods:
Tree edit distance
symbol overlap
functional equivalence test
Example similarity score:
$$
S(f_t, f_{t-1}) \in [0,1]
$$
Low similarity ⇒ structural drift.
---
Step 4 — coefficient monitoring
If structure identical:
Track coefficient vectors:
$$
\beta_t
$$
Drift detection:
$$
||\beta_t - \beta_{t-1}||
$$
or
CUSUM / EWMA monitoring.
---
4. Weighted formula idea (what you suggested)
Your idea is essentially temporal ensemble symbolic regression.
Let daily models be:
$$
f_1, f_2, ..., f_t
$$
Construct weighted predictor:
$$
f^*(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{K} w_k f_{t-k}(x)
$$
Where:
$$
\sum w_k = 1
$$
Example weights:
Exponential decay:
$$
w_k = \lambda^k
$$
normalized.
Interpretation:
recent formulas influence prediction more
older formulas stabilize noise.
---
5. Alternative (often better)
Instead of fitting separate daily models, fit on sliding window data.
Example:
$$
D_{window} = \bigcup_{k=0}^{K} D_{t-k}
$$
Then:
$$
f_t = SR(D_{window})
$$
Advantages:
reduces noise
drift detection becomes smoother.
---
6. More robust drift detection
A stronger method:
Maintain a formula archive
$$
\mathcal{F} = {f_1, f_2, ..., f_t}
$$
Evaluate each on latest data:
formula	error on $D_t$
$f_{t-5}$	0.04
$f_{t-3}$	0.05
$f_{t-1}$	0.22
$f_t$	0.04
Interpretation:
old formula still works → no drift
only new formula works → drift occurred
---
7. Detecting functional drift directly
Instead of comparing formulas, compare function outputs.
Sample points:
$$
X = {x_1,...,x_m}
$$
Evaluate:
$$
\Delta = \frac{1}{m}\sum |f_t(x_i) - f_{t-1}(x_i)|
$$
Large $\Delta$ ⇒ drift.
This avoids symbolic comparison complexity.
---
8. Practical architecture
A simple system:
```
Daily pipeline
--------------
1. Ingest data D_t
2. Fit symbolic regression -> f_t
3. Store:
   - formula
   - coefficients
   - training error
4. Evaluate:
   - f_t on historical data
   - historical formulas on D_t
5. Drift tests:
   - error change
   - formula similarity
   - coefficient shift
6. Update ensemble predictor
```
---
9. Key practical challenge
Symbolic regression is high variance.
Small data changes often produce different expressions even if the underlying function is identical.
Mitigations:
complexity penalties
pareto front selection
model simplification
stability selection.
---
10. A more statistically grounded formulation
You are implicitly estimating a time-varying functional relationship:
$$
y_t = f_t(x_t) + \epsilon_t
$$
and testing:
$$
H_0 : f_t = f_{t-k}
$$
versus
$$
H_1 : f_t \neq f_{t-k}
$$
Symbolic regression provides an interpretable representation of $f_t$.
---
11. A design that works well in practice
A practical approach is:
Fit symbolic regression on rolling window
Maintain Pareto frontier of formulas
Track:
error on holdout stream
expression similarity
coefficient change
4.  Trigger drift if:
$$
E_{t}(f_{t-k}) - E_t(f_t) > \delta
$$
for several $k$.

1. Learning mechanism
Symbolic regression
Most implementations use evolutionary search.
Typical process:
Generate random expressions.
Evaluate fitness on data.
Apply genetic operations:
mutation
crossover
simplification.
Select best candidates.
Objective:
$$
\min_f L(f(X),y) + \lambda \cdot \text{complexity}(f)
$$
Search space = all possible formulas.
---
Random forest
Random forests follow a deterministic training procedure:
Draw bootstrap samples.
Grow decision trees.
At each split choose a random subset of features.
Objective per split:
$$
\min \text{MSE}
$$
No search over mathematical forms occurs.

Symbolic regression
Produces a continuous mathematical relationship.
Example:
$$
f(x) = x_1^2 + \sin(x_2)
$$
Properties:
smooth
differentiable (often)
compact.
---
Random forest
Function approximated as piecewise regions.
Example conceptual representation:
$$
f(x) = \begin{cases} 3.2 & x_1 < 1.2 \ 4.7 & x_1 \ge 1.2 \end{cases}
$$
Forest smooths these discontinuities through averaging.
---
1. Interpretability
Symbolic regression
Interpretability arises from the formula itself.
Example:
$$
y = ax + b
$$
You can directly infer:
variable relationships
scaling behaviour
nonlinear mechanisms.
This is often used for scientific discovery.

If the goal is to produce a symbolic formula from a random forest, the typical method is model distillation or symbolic surrogate modelling.
The idea is:
$$
f_{SR}(x) \approx f_{RF}(x)
$$
where
$f_{RF}$ = trained random forest
$f_{SR}$ = symbolic regression model approximating it.
The symbolic model learns the functional behaviour of the forest, not the original data directly.
---
1. Why this is done
Random forests provide:
strong predictive accuracy
robustness to noise.
Symbolic regression provides:
interpretable functional structure
compact mathematical representation.
Combining them produces a high-accuracy model with a simplified analytical description.
---
1. Distillation pipeline
Step 1 — train the random forest
Train on dataset:
$$
D = {(x_i, y_i)}
$$
Model:
$$
f_{RF}(x)
$$
---
Step 2 — generate synthetic training data
Sample inputs from the feature space.
Example:
$$
X^{} = {x_1^, x_2^, ..., x_m^}
$$
Compute labels using the forest:
$$
y_i^* = f_{RF}(x_i^*)
$$
New dataset:
$$
D^* = (X^, y^)
$$
This dataset represents the response surface learned by the forest.
---
Step 3 — run symbolic regression
Train symbolic regression on:
$$
(X^, y^)
$$
Objective:
$$
\min_f \sum (f(x_i^) - y_i^)^2 + \lambda \cdot complexity(f)
$$
Result:
$$
f_{SR}(x)
$$
---
1. Interpretation
The symbolic formula becomes an analytical approximation of the ensemble model.
Example:
Random forest behaviour:
```
complex nonlinear interactions
```
Symbolic regression approximation:
$$
y \approx 1.7x_1 + 0.9x_2^2 - 0.3\log(x_3)
$$
This reveals:
dominant variables
nonlinear relationships.
---
1. Benefits
2. Model compression
A forest with hundreds of trees becomes a single formula.
Example:
```
Random forest: 500 trees
Symbolic formula: 8 terms
```
---
1. Interpretability
A symbolic model exposes relationships such as:
polynomial growth
logarithmic scaling
interaction terms.
---
1. Analytical manipulation
Because the model is explicit:
You can compute:
derivatives
$$
\frac{\partial y}{\partial x_i}
$$
integrals
asymptotic behaviour.
These are impossible with forests.
---
1. Limitations
2. Approximation error
Symbolic regression cannot always perfectly reproduce the forest surface.
Complex forests may require large formulas.
---
1. Feature interactions
Forests can capture extremely complex interaction boundaries that symbolic regression may simplify.
---
1. Stability
Symbolic approximations may vary depending on:
sampling strategy
search randomness.
---
1. Example workflow
Example implementation structure:
```
# train random forest
rf.fit(X, y)

# sample feature space
X_sample = sample_features()

# generate predictions
y_sample = rf.predict(X_sample)

# run symbolic regression
model = pysr.fit(X_sample, y_sample)
```

$$
f_{SR}(x)

# Symbolic formula against the random forest predictions comparison

Response:
#ml #symbolic_regression #random_forest #model_comparison #evaluation

When comparing a produced symbolic formula with a random forest, the main question is whether the symbolic model adequately approximates the predictive behaviour of the forest.
Formally:
random forest model: $f_{RF}(x)$
symbolic formula: $f_{SR}(x)$
The comparison evaluates how well
$$
f_{SR}(x) \approx f_{RF}(x)
$$
over the relevant input space.
---
1. Prediction agreement
The most direct comparison is the prediction difference.
Construct a dataset $X^{*}$ sampled from the feature distribution.
Compute:
$$
y_{RF} = f_{RF}(X^{*})
$$
$$
y_{SR} = f_{SR}(X^{*})
$$
Then measure error:
Mean squared error
$$
MSE = \frac{1}{n}\sum (y_{RF} - y_{SR})^2
$$
Mean absolute error
$$
MAE = \frac{1}{n}\sum |y_{RF} - y_{SR}|
$$
These measure how closely the symbolic formula reproduces the forest.
---
2. Functional similarity
Because forests produce complex surfaces, it is useful to evaluate functional distance.
Define:
$$
\Delta = \mathbb{E}x[|f{RF}(x) - f_{SR}(x)|]
$$
In practice this expectation is approximated by sampling.
Small $\Delta$ indicates the symbolic model is a good surrogate.
---
3. Behavioural comparison
Even if overall error is small, the models may differ in certain regions.
Useful diagnostics:
Partial dependence comparison
For feature $x_j$:
$$
PD_{RF}(x_j)
$$
$$
PD_{SR}(x_j)
$$
Plot both curves and inspect whether the symbolic formula reproduces the same trend.
---
Interaction behaviour
Random forests often capture complex interactions such as
$$
x_1 \times x_2
$$
The symbolic formula may approximate this interaction explicitly.
Example:
Forest behaviour:
```
x1 and x2 jointly influence output
```
Symbolic approximation:
$$
y = 1.3x_1 + 0.8x_2 + 0.5x_1x_2
$$
---
4. Out-of-sample comparison
A critical check is evaluating both models on true data rather than synthetic samples.
Compute:
$$
R^2_{RF}
$$
$$
R^2_{SR}
$$
on validation data.
Typical pattern:
model	$R^2$
Random forest	0.92
Symbolic regression	0.84
The symbolic model usually sacrifices some predictive accuracy.
---
5. Complexity comparison
Another comparison dimension is model size.
Example:
Random forest:
```
400 trees
depth ≈ 10
~10000 decision nodes
```
Symbolic formula:
$$
y = 2.1x_1 + 0.3x_2^2 - 0.8\log(x_3)
$$
Complexity metric often used:
$$
C(f) = \text{number of operators + variables}
$$
Symbolic models are dramatically smaller.
---
6. Extrapolation comparison
Symbolic formulas generalize outside the training range.
Example:
Training domain:
$$
x \in [0,10]
$$
Prediction at $x=20$:
forest: returns value similar to training region
symbolic formula: follows mathematical growth.
Therefore behaviour can diverge significantly.
---
7. Stability comparison
Another useful measure is sensitivity to data changes.
Random forests typically produce stable predictions.
Symbolic regression can produce different formulas even if predictive behaviour is similar.
Example:
$$
f_{SR1}(x) = 2x + 3
$$
$$
f_{SR2}(x) = 1.9x + 3.1
$$
Different expressions but nearly identical predictions.
---
8. Residual comparison
Evaluate residuals against the forest:
$$
r(x) = f_{RF}(x) - f_{SR}(x)
$$
Plotting residuals reveals where the symbolic model fails.
Typical pattern:
symbolic model captures global trend
forest captures local irregularities.
---
9. Interpretation difference
Random forest represents the function as a large set of region-based rules.
Symbolic regression represents the function as a single analytical relationship.
When the symbolic approximation works well, it suggests the forest’s behaviour can be explained by a relatively simple underlying relationship.
---
A useful extension, particularly relevant to your earlier concept-drift idea, is to track changes in the symbolic approximation of the forest over time:
$$
f_{SR,t}(x) \approx f_{RF,t}(x)
$$
Drift could then be interpreted as changes in the symbolic structure rather than changes inside the forest ensemble.
Two design choices affect this strongly:
whether the symbolic regression approximates the forest predictions or the original data
how densely the feature space is sampled when generating surrogate training data.

#ml #symbolic_regression #random_forest #local_models #model_interpretability #surrogate_models
Yes. Instead of fitting one global symbolic expression
$$
f_{SR}(x)
$$
you can construct local symbolic models
$$
f_{SR}^{(r)}(x)
$$
for regions $r$ of the feature space and compare those against the behaviour of the random forest.
This approach often works better because random forests inherently model local structure.
---
1. Conceptual structure
Random forest prediction:
$$
f_{RF}(x)
$$
Partition feature space into regions:
$$
R_1, R_2, \dots, R_k
$$
Within each region fit a symbolic model:
$$
f_{SR}^{(i)}(x) \quad \text{for } x \in R_i
$$
This yields a piecewise symbolic model:
$$
f(x) = \begin{cases} f_{SR}^{(1)}(x) & x \in R_1 \ f_{SR}^{(2)}(x) & x \in R_2 \ \dots \end{cases}
$$
---
2. Why this matches forests better
Random forests approximate functions as piecewise regions.
Each tree splits the space:
```
x1 < 3.2
x2 > 1.4
```
Predictions inside those regions are simple averages.
Therefore the forest surface is inherently locally simple but globally complex.
Local symbolic regression exploits that property.
---
3. Method 1 — region partitioning from the forest
One strategy is to use the forest itself to define regions.
Procedure:
Train random forest.
Extract leaf regions from trees.
Cluster similar leaves into larger regions.
Fit symbolic regression inside each region.
Example region:
$$
R_1 = {x : x_1 < 3, x_2 > 1.5}
$$
Symbolic model inside region:
$$
y = 1.8x_1 + 0.4x_2
$$
---
4. Method 2 — clustering the feature space
Another approach:
Cluster observations
$$
X \rightarrow \text{k clusters}
$$
2.  Fit symbolic regression inside each cluster.
Example:
Cluster 1 model:
$$
y = 2x_1 + 0.5x_2
$$
Cluster 2 model:
$$
y = \log(x_1) + 3x_3
$$
This produces region-specific functional relationships.
---
5. Method 3 — neighbourhood models (LIME-style)
For a given point $x_0$:
Sample points near $x_0$.
Evaluate random forest predictions.
Fit symbolic regression locally.
Result:
$$
f_{SR}^{(x_0)}(x)
$$
This approximates the local gradient structure of the forest.
---
6. Comparing local symbolic models to the forest
Within region $R_i$:
Evaluate approximation error:
$$
E_i = \frac{1}{n_i} \sum_{x \in R_i} (f_{RF}(x) - f_{SR}^{(i)}(x))^2
$$
Low error means the forest behaviour is locally explainable.
---
7. Advantages of local symbolic models
Better approximation
Global symbolic models struggle with highly nonlinear forests.
Local models capture simpler relationships.
Example:
Global relationship (forest):
```
piecewise nonlinear surface
```
Local models:
Region 1:
$$
y = 2x_1 + 0.3x_2
$$
Region 2:
$$
y = x_1^2 + 1.2x_3
$$
---
Interpretability
You obtain context-dependent rules:
```
If x1 < 3 and x2 > 1:
    y ≈ 2x1 + 0.5x2
```
---
Drift detection
For your earlier concept drift idea, local models allow monitoring:
$$
f_{SR,t}^{(i)}(x)
$$
Changes in regional formulas can reveal local concept drift.
Example:
Week 1 region model:
$$
y = 1.5x_1 + 0.4x_2
$$
Week 8 region model:
$$
y = 2.3x_1 + 0.4x_2
$$
Only coefficient changed in that region.
---
8. Limitation
Too many regions creates instability.
Typical design:
5–20 regions
symbolic complexity constraint
minimum sample size per region.
---
9. Resulting hybrid model
The final system often becomes:
$$
f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathbf{1}{x \in R_i} f{SR}^{(i)}(x)
$$
This is effectively a rule-based symbolic model approximating the forest.
